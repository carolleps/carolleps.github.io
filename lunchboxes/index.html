Before You Write

What's Your Angle?

A speech about lunchboxes might sound simple — but done well, it's actually one of the most personal and powerful things you could talk about. Here's why:

The Big Idea: Your lunchbox is not just food. It's a story. It carries the memory of where your family came from, what your grandmother learned to cook, how your culture survived a journey to a new country. And when you open it at school — you're sharing all of that, even if nobody realises it. The lunchbox is one of the most honest windows into who someone really is.

There are a few different directions you can take this speech. Which one feels most true to you?

AThe lunchbox as identity — what it says about where you come from without a single word
BThe "weird food" moment — when someone reacts to your food, and what that really reveals about curiosity vs judgement
CFood as a bridge — how sharing food across cultures creates connection that nothing else can
DWhat happens when you look around the lunch table — and realise you're looking at the whole world in miniature
Step 1

Think Before You Write

Grab a notebook. These questions are designed to unlock real memories and specific details — which is exactly what makes a speech memorable.

🥡 About Your Own Lunchbox
→What do you usually have in your lunchbox? Where does that food come from — which culture, which family member's kitchen?
→Is there a food your family makes that you've never seen anywhere else? What's the story behind it?
→Has anyone ever reacted to something in your lunchbox — positively or negatively? How did that feel?
→Is there a food that, when you smell it, takes you somewhere — a memory, a person, a place?
👀 About What You've Noticed Around You
→What's the most interesting thing you've ever seen in someone else's lunchbox? Did you ask about it?
→Have you ever tried food from another culture and been surprised — either because you loved it or because it was nothing like you expected?
→Have you ever seen someone feel embarrassed about their food at school? What do you think caused that — and was it fair?
→When you look at what people eat at lunch, what does it tell you about the diversity of your school?
🌍 About the Bigger Picture
→Why do you think food carries culture so strongly — even across generations, even after migration?
→What would it mean if everyone brought the same sandwich every day? What would be lost?
→Is there a connection between being curious about someone's food and being curious about their culture more broadly?
Tip: The best moment in your speech will be a specific, real memory — not a general idea. Pick one food, one moment, one person and build a scene around it. That's what people remember.
Step 2

Build Your Speech — 6 Parts

Six parts, each with a clear job. Click to open each one.

1
The Hook — Open With a Scene, Not a Statement
~30 seconds · 50–60 words
▼
Put the audience right into a moment — they should see and smell it before you've explained anything
"Picture this: it's lunchtime. You open your lunchbox. There's a smell. Maybe it's rice and fish. Maybe it's dolmades. Maybe it's a perfectly wrapped banh mi. Across the table, someone stares. They've never seen that before. In that moment — something important is about to happen."
"My grandmother never learned to cook Australian food. She said she didn't need to. Everything she knew — everything she was — was already in the food she made. And somehow, it ended up in my lunchbox."
"Every lunchbox at this school tells a story. Most people never ask to hear it. I think that's a mistake."
2
Food as Identity — What's Really in the Box
~45 seconds · 75–85 words
▼
Explain your big idea: food carries culture, history, and identity in a way that's almost impossible to fake
Think about: A recipe that survived a migration. A dish made the same way for three generations. The smell that means "home." Food doesn't just taste like a culture — it IS a culture, preserved and passed down.
Example paragraph

"When my [family member] makes [a dish], they're not just cooking. They're carrying something across time — a recipe that came from [a place], survived [a journey or history], and landed in our kitchen in Australia. When I eat it, I'm connected to people and places I've never been. That's not just food. That's identity."

3
The "Weird Food" Moment — Curiosity vs Judgement
~60 seconds · 100–110 words
▼
Explore what happens when someone sees unfamiliar food — and what that reaction reveals about them
This is the most honest part of your speech. Have you ever felt embarrassed about your food? Have you ever seen someone else feel that way? Have you ever been the one who reacted poorly — and later wished you hadn't? This section is about what "weird" really means, and why curiosity is always the better response.
Example paragraph

"There's a moment that happens in almost every multicultural school. Someone opens their lunchbox and someone else says — 'Ew, what IS that?' It's usually not meant to be cruel. But it lands like a small rejection. Not just of the food, but of the family who made it, the culture it came from, the person who brought it. Curiosity would have sounded different: 'What's that? Can I try it?' That one question changes everything."

4
Food as a Bridge — What Sharing Creates
~55 seconds · 90–100 words
▼
Show the positive side — what happens when people share food across cultures, and what it builds
Think about: a time someone offered you a bite of something unfamiliar and you said yes. Or a time you offered yours. What happened next? Sharing food is one of the oldest forms of human trust — and it still works.
Example paragraph

"The year I became friends with [someone from a different background], it started with food. They offered me a piece of [something]. I had no idea what it was. But I said yes. It tasted like nothing I'd ever had. And somehow, in that moment, I understood something about them that no introduction could have given me. Food skips the awkward part. It goes straight to the good bit."

5
The Lunch Table — A World in Miniature
~40 seconds · 65–75 words
▼
Zoom out — connect your personal experience to the bigger picture of what a multicultural school or country looks like
"If you look around the lunch tables at this school, you'll find traces of every continent. Not all at once — but across all of us, scattered through a hundred lunchboxes, there's a version of the whole world. That's not an accident. That's Australia."
"The United Nations has 193 member countries. Our lunch tables probably don't reach that far. But I'd bet we're closer than most people realise."
6
The Closing — One Last Thing to Carry Home
~30 seconds · 50–60 words
▼
Land it cleanly — one clear, lasting idea. Try to echo your opening if you can.
"Next time someone opens a lunchbox near you and you don't recognise what's inside — don't look away. Ask. Because behind that food is a story. And stories are always worth hearing."
"Food is never just food. It's memory, it's belonging, it's the long journey of a family to a new place, packed into a container and carried here. Every lunchbox is a small act of culture. Open yours with pride."
"Multiculturalism isn't a policy. It isn't a school assembly. It's a Tuesday lunchtime. And it's been here all along."
Step 3

Word Count by Part

4 minutes = roughly 450–500 words at a clear, unhurried pace. Here's how to distribute them.

Target Words Per Section
1 · Hook
~55 words
2 · Food as Identity
~80 words
3 · Weird Food Moment
~105 words
4 · Food as Bridge
~95 words
5 · The Lunch Table
~70 words

~55 words
Remember: Read your draft out loud and time it. A confident speaker does around 120–130 words per minute. If you're at 3 minutes — add more to Part 3 or Part 4. If you're over 5 — tighten your Part 2. Your ears are a better editor than your eyes.

Step 5

Delivering It Well

The writing is 60% of the work. Delivery is the other 40%. Here's how to do it justice.

🎭
Let the scenes be scenes
When you describe the lunchbox moment — slow down. Paint the picture. Let the audience see it before you explain it.
👁️
Look up at the personal parts
When you get to your own story, put the paper down if you can. Looking at the audience when you speak from experience makes it land much harder.
⏸️
Pause after the important line
If you've written something you're proud of — pause after it. Give the audience two seconds to feel it before moving on.
🗣️
Don't rush the "weird food" part
That section has the most emotional weight. Speak it slowly and clearly — it's the moment the audience really listens.
📝
Know your first and last lines
Memorise your hook and your closing. Starting and ending without looking at the page signals total confidence.
💛
This speech is yours
Nobody else in that room has your food, your memories, your story. That's your advantage. Use it.
