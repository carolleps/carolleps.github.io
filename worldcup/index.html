Before You Write

What's Your Angle?

The World Cup is the biggest sporting event on Earth. But your speech isn't really about football — it's about what football reveals about multiculturalism. Here's the big idea:

The Big Idea: For one month every four years, 5 billion people — more than half the world's population — watch the same event. Different languages, different religions, different histories. All united by one sport. The World Cup doesn't erase our differences. It puts them on full display — and that's exactly what makes it beautiful.

You can take your speech in different directions. Think about which one excites you most:

A Football as a universal language — the one thing that needs no translation
B What we learn about a country by watching how they play — their style, their passion, their identity
C What happens when fans from rival countries end up in the same crowd — and become friends anyway
D The players themselves — many grew up in two or three countries, representing the complexity of modern identity
Step 1

Think Before You Write

Grab a notebook. Answer as many of these as you can. The best speeches come from real, specific memories — not general statements.

🌍 About Football and Culture
→Have you ever watched a World Cup match? Which one? What do you remember most about it?
→Do you have a team you support? Why that team — is it family, heritage, something else?
→Have you ever watched a match with people who were barracking for the other team? What was that like?
→Is there a player from another country whose story you find interesting or inspiring?
→Have you ever noticed how different countries play differently — different styles, energy, tactics?
🤔 About Multiculturalism and Sport
→Why do you think billions of people care so much about a sport that's just 22 people kicking a ball?
→Many players in the World Cup represent countries they weren't even born in — what does that say about identity?
→Can you think of a moment in football history where sport brought people together across cultural divides?
→Australia's Socceroos squad has players with backgrounds from over 20 different countries — what does that tell you about us?
→What would the world look like if more things worked the way the World Cup does — everyone competing fairly, then going home?
Tip: Pick one specific moment — a match you watched, a player you admire, a conversation you had — and build your speech around it. Specific stories are always more powerful than general ideas.
Step 2

Build Your Speech — 6 Parts

A 4-minute speech needs more depth than a short one. Six parts, each with a clear job to do. Click each one to open it.

01
The Hook — Make Them Stop and Listen
~30 seconds · 50–60 words
▼
Drop the audience into a moment — a sound, a statistic, a question — before they realise you've started
"Imagine 5 billion people — more than half of everyone alive on this planet — all watching the same 90 minutes. Different languages. Different foods on the table. Different flags on the wall. The same game."
"The last time Australia played France in the World Cup, there were fans in the same stadium who had never met anyone from the other's country. By full time, some of them were hugging."
"There is one event on Earth that can stop a war. It happened in 1914. Christmas Day. A football match, no-man's land, and two sides who had been trying to kill each other — playing together."
02
Set the Scene — What IS the World Cup?
~40 seconds · 70–80 words
▼
Give your audience just enough context — but make it interesting, not a Wikipedia entry
Explain the scale: 32 nations, years of qualification, one trophy. Then flip to the human side: the fans who travel, the families watching from living rooms in countries too small to qualify, the players who grew up in two countries and must choose one to represent.
Example paragraph

"The FIFA World Cup happens every four years. 32 countries qualify. Millions more don't. But on the day of the final, an estimated 5 billion people tune in — not just fans of the two teams playing, but people around the world who understand that something bigger than football is happening."

03
First Big Idea — The Language That Needs No Translation
~60 seconds · 100–110 words
▼
Argue that football works across cultures because its rules are universal — and explore what that really means

Think about: You don't need to speak the same language to understand a goal. You don't need to share the same religion to feel the same devastation when a penalty is missed. This is rare in the world — and worth exploring.

Example paragraph

"I've watched matches with my [family member / friend] who speaks almost no English. But when the goal goes in, we react exactly the same way. We don't need words. Football is the one conversation that works without a translator. That's not just sport — that's a miracle of human connection."

04
Second Big Idea — What Their Game Tells You About Their Culture
~60 seconds · 100–110 words
▼
Show how watching different countries play is actually a crash course in their culture, values and identity

Think about: Brazilian football is expressive, joyful, improvised — jogo bonito, the beautiful game. German football is disciplined, structured, relentless. Japanese teams are known for honour — they famously cleaned their dressing room after being eliminated. These aren't accidents — they reflect something real about who these countries are.

Example paragraph

"When Japan was eliminated from the 2022 World Cup, their players cleaned the entire dressing room and left an origami crane. When Morocco reached the semi-finals — the first African nation ever to do so — their players dedicated it to their mothers. These moments don't just tell you about football. They tell you about people."

05
Personal Connection — Bring It Back to You
~40 seconds · 70–80 words
▼
Connect the big ideas to your own experience — this is what makes a speech memorable, not just informative

Tell a specific story — even a small one. Who did you watch a match with? What did you feel? Which player's story resonated with you and why? You don't need a dramatic story — an honest one is enough.

Example paragraph

"I remember watching [a match / the 2023 Women's World Cup in Australia] with [someone]. What stayed with me wasn't the score. It was [a specific moment — a reaction, a chant, something you noticed]. In that moment, I realised that sport was showing me something I hadn't learned in any classroom."

06
The Closing — Leave Them Thinking
~30 seconds · 50–60 words
▼
End strong — circle back to your hook if you can, and leave the audience with one lasting idea
"The World Cup ends every four years. The flags come down. The fans go home. But something stays — a little bit more understanding, a little more curiosity, a little less fear of the unfamiliar. That's not just sport. That's multiculturalism in action."
"Five billion people watching together. Different languages, different flags, different histories — all holding their breath at the same moment. Maybe the World Cup doesn't just show us how different we are. Maybe it shows us how much we have in common."
Step 3

Word Count by Part

4 minutes = roughly 450–500 words spoken at a clear, confident pace. Here's how to spread them across your speech.

Target Word Count Per Part
01 · Hook
~55 words
02 · Set the Scene
~75 words
03 · First Big Idea
~105 words
04 · Second Big Idea
~105 words
05 · Personal Story
~75 words
06 · Closing
~55 words
Remember: These are targets, not rules. If your personal story needs more space, give it more. If your hook is landing perfectly at 40 words, don't pad it. Read the whole thing out loud and time it — that's the only real test.
Step 4

Draft Checklist

Once you've written your draft, go through these. Tick each one honestly.

0 / 9
✓
My opening sentence creates a strong image or feeling before I've explained anything
✓
I've used at least one real, specific fact (a statistic, a country, a player, a moment)
✓
My first big idea is clearly argued — not just described
✓
My second big idea adds something NEW — not just repeating the first point
✓
I've included a personal connection or story — something that is genuinely mine
✓
My closing ties back to my opening or leaves a clear final thought
✓
I've read it out loud and it runs 3.5 to 4.5 minutes
✓
There are no sentences so long that I run out of breath saying them
✓
It sounds like me — not like an encyclopedia article
Step 5

Delivering It Well

The writing gets you 60% of the way there. The delivery is the other 40%.

⏱️
Pace yourself early
The first 30 seconds determine everything. Go slower than feels comfortable — your nerves will naturally speed you up.
👁️
Pick three people
Find three friendly faces in the room and rotate eye contact between them. It looks natural and keeps you grounded.
⏸️
Use silence
After a powerful sentence, pause for two seconds. Silence is not failure — it's emphasis. It makes the line land harder.
📝
Know your opening cold
Memorise just the first two sentences. Starting without looking down builds instant confidence and credibility.
💪
Stand tall
Feet shoulder-width apart, weight evenly distributed. Your body language tells the audience whether to trust you before you say a word.
🎯
Own your personal story
The part about YOU is the only part no one else can give this speech. Slow down there — that's the part worth hearing.

The Whistle Blows.
You're Ready.

Five billion people find a way to care about the same thing at the same time. That's what you're writing about. Now go tell that story — in your words, your voice, your way.
